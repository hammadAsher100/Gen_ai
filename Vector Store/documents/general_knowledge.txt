# General Knowledge: History, Science, and the Natural World

## A Brief History of Human Civilization

### The Ancient World

Human civilization is generally considered to have begun around 3500 BCE with the rise of the Sumerian civilization in Mesopotamia (modern-day Iraq). The Sumerians developed one of the earliest writing systems — cuneiform — inscribed on clay tablets. They built city-states like Ur and Uruk, established legal codes, and developed early mathematics.

Parallel civilizations flourished along other major rivers:
- **Ancient Egypt** along the Nile (c. 3100 BCE): Known for monumental architecture, hieroglyphics, and complex religious systems.
- **Indus Valley Civilization** (c. 2600 BCE): One of the world's earliest urban cultures, with advanced city planning in sites like Mohenjo-daro and Harappa.
- **Ancient China** along the Yellow River (c. 2000 BCE): Developed independently, producing bronze metallurgy, silk production, and early written characters.

### Classical Antiquity

The period from roughly 800 BCE to 600 CE is known as Classical Antiquity, dominated by Greek and Roman civilizations.

**Ancient Greece** (c. 800–146 BCE):
- Birthplace of democracy, philosophy, and Western science
- Key thinkers: Socrates, Plato, Aristotle
- Cultural achievements: the Olympics, tragedy and comedy in theater, advances in mathematics and astronomy

**The Roman Empire** (27 BCE – 476 CE):
- Expanded across Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East
- Contributions: Roman law, engineering (aqueducts, roads), the Latin language
- Divided into Western and Eastern (Byzantine) empires; the Western fell in 476 CE

## Key Scientific Discoveries

### The Scientific Revolution (16th–17th Century)

The Scientific Revolution transformed humanity's understanding of the natural world:

- **Nicolaus Copernicus** (1543): Proposed a heliocentric model of the solar system, displacing Earth from the center.
- **Galileo Galilei**: Used a telescope to observe moons of Jupiter, supporting the Copernican model.
- **Isaac Newton** (1687): Formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation in *Principia Mathematica*, unifying celestial and terrestrial mechanics.
- **Francis Bacon**: Developed the empirical method, emphasizing observation and experimentation as the basis for knowledge.

### The Theory of Evolution

Charles Darwin published *On the Origin of Species* in 1859, proposing that species evolve through natural selection. Key concepts:
- Variation exists within species
- Traits that improve survival are passed on
- Over generations, populations change — a process called evolution
- All life on Earth shares common ancestors

Darwin's theory, supported by fossil records, genetics, and molecular biology, remains the foundational framework of modern biology.

### Quantum Mechanics and Relativity

The 20th century brought two revolutionary theories that upended classical physics:

**Special and General Relativity (Einstein, 1905–1915)**:
- Mass and energy are equivalent: E = mc²
- Gravity is the curvature of spacetime caused by mass
- Time passes more slowly near massive objects (time dilation)

**Quantum Mechanics**:
- Developed by Bohr, Heisenberg, Schrödinger, and others
- Describes the behavior of matter at atomic and subatomic scales
- Introduces probabilistic rather than deterministic descriptions
- Underpins technologies like lasers, transistors, and MRI scanners

## The Natural World

### Earth's Structure

The Earth is composed of four main layers:
1. **Inner Core**: Solid iron and nickel; approximately 1,220 km radius
2. **Outer Core**: Liquid iron and nickel; generates Earth's magnetic field
3. **Mantle**: Semi-solid rock; convection currents drive tectonic plate movement
4. **Crust**: Thin outermost layer; 5–70 km thick; supports all life

### Plate Tectonics

The theory of plate tectonics explains that Earth's lithosphere is divided into large plates that move over the asthenosphere. This movement causes:
- Earthquakes (at fault lines)
- Volcanic activity (at divergent and convergent boundaries)
- Mountain formation (at collision zones, e.g., the Himalayas)
- Seafloor spreading and continental drift
